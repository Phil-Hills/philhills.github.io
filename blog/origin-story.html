Protocol by Accident

How a hatred of cloud bills birthed the Q-Protocol.
Feb 2026 · Phil Hills · 4 min read

I didn't set out to invent a high-frequency agent orchestration standard. I wasn't trying to revolutionize "Swarm Intelligence" or write a whitepaper. I was just looking at my API bill and getting angry.

Here’s the thing about me: I hate paying rent to tech giants.

Especially Google and AWS. It’s a visceral thing. When I run an agent swarm, and I see thousands of JSON objects flying back and forth—curly braces, key names, redundant whitespace—all I see is my money burning. I see a "Token Tax" on every single character.

The $50 Problem

I was prototyping a multi-agent system (what eventually became the Agentic Empire). I had about 15 agents trying to coordinate a simple task. They were chatting using standard English + JSON:

"Agent A to Agent B: Please confirm you have received the payload. Status: CONFIRMED."

It was polite. It was readable. And it was ruinously expensive.

Every "Please" cost me tokens. Every "Status: CONFIRMED" was redundant data appearing in hundreds of logs. My bill was climbing past $50 just for the agents to say "hello" to each other.

"Why are they talking like humans?"

That was the lightbulb moment. Agents aren't human. They don't need politeness. They don't even need words. They need coordinates.

I realized that an agent's state isn't a paragraph of text; it's a vector. It's a point in space. "Idle" is a 0. "Working" is a 1. "Error" is a 2.

If I could map all these states into a 3D coordinate system, I wouldn't need to send JSON objects like { "status": "active", "task": "search" }. I could just send a single integer.

Enter Z-Order Curves

I grabbed an old concept from computer graphics called Morton Coding (Z-Order curves). It’s a way to squash multi-dimensional data (X, Y, Z) into a single number while preserving locality.

I ripped out the JSON layer. I ripped out the English logic.

Instead of "chatting," my agents started broadcasting Integers. A single 64-bit hash that represented who they were, what they were doing, and where they were in memory.

The Accidental Protocol

The result was the Q-Protocol (Query-Stream).

Suddenly, my 15-agent swarm wasn't generating megabytes of text logs. It was generating a tiny, hum of binary data. The "noise" was gone.

The stats were hilarious:

  • Payload Size: Reduced by 40x (40:1 compression).
  • Latency: Dropped from 400ms to near-instant.
  • Cost: That $50/month projection? It dropped to pennies.

I accidentally invented a high-performance, cryptographically verifiable, spatially hashed telemetry standard... solely because I was too cheap to pay for JSON tokens.

And honestly? That's the best way to engineer meaningful systems. Constraints breed creativity. And nothing constrains you quite like refusing to give a billionaire another dime of your money.
